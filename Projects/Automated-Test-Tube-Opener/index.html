Client-Sponsored Engineering Design

Designed and built a proof-of-concept laboratory automation device for Duke University's David Lab capable of simultaneously removing eight test tube caps. The system reduces repetitive strain, minimizes cross-contamination, and improves laboratory efficiency through automated mechanical actuation.

Project Overview

The Problem

Researchers in Duke University's David Lab manually opened and reattached hundreds of test tube caps during routine sample preparation.

This resulted in:

  • Repetitive wrist and forearm fatigue
  • Greater risk of cross-contamination from placing caps on the bench
  • Over 1 hour to process just 20 test tubes

Design Requirements

  • Open 8 laboratory tubes simultaneously
  • Reduce processing time by at least 50%
  • Hold caps after removal to prevent contamination
  • Operate hands-free after cycle initiation
  • Fit within a 12" × 16" laboratory footprint
  • Provide safe, repeatable operation

Mechanical Design

I led the CAD design and mechanical development of the automated tube opener, focusing on reliable cap removal while maintaining laboratory safety and minimizing cross-contamination. The system combines a spring-loaded gripper, rack-and-pinion transport mechanism, custom silicone tube holders, and a fully 3D printed PLA structure into a compact automated device.

Spring Gripper

Spring-loaded mechanism securely grips each cap while a threaded shaft converts motor rotation into simultaneous upward translation and cap removal.

Rack & Pinion

A geared DC motor drives a rack-and-pinion system that moves the tube holder smoothly into and out of the machine.

Tube Retention

Custom silicone moldings securely hold each test tube in place while preventing unwanted rotation.

Manufacturing

Entire mechanism designed in SolidWorks and fabricated using FDM 3D printed PLA components.
