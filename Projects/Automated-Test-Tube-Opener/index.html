Automatic Test Tube Opener

Client-Sponsored Engineering Project

Designed and built an automated laboratory device for Duke University's David Lab that removes eight test tube caps simultaneously.

David Lab • EGR 101 • Fall 2025 • Team of 4

Project Overview

The Problem

Researchers in Duke University's David Lab manually opened and reattached hundreds of test tube caps during routine sample preparation, creating a slow, repetitive workflow.

This resulted in:

  • Repetitive wrist and forearm fatigue
  • Greater risk of cross-contamination from placing caps on the bench
  • Over 1 hour to process just 20 test tubes

Design Requirements

  • Open 8 laboratory tubes simultaneously
  • Reduce processing time by at least 50%
  • Hold caps after removal to prevent contamination
  • Operate hands-free after cycle initiation
  • Fit within a 12" × 16" laboratory footprint
  • Provide safe, repeatable operation

Mechanical Design

I was responsible for the CAD, mechanical design, and assembly of the tube transport mechanism, rack-and-pinion drive, and portions of the cap gripper. The design emphasized reliable tube positioning, manufacturability, and rapid prototyping using 3D-printed PLA components.

Rack & Pinion

Designed a motor-driven rack-and-pinion mechanism that moved eight test tubes into and out of the machine during each cycle.

Spring Gripper

Developed a spring-loaded gripper that securely grasped each tube cap while allowing it to travel upward as the cap unscrewed.

Silicone Tube Holders

Created custom silicone inserts that prevented the tubes from rotating while protecting delicate laboratory glassware.

Rapid Prototyping

Iteratively redesigned and 3D printed PLA components to improve alignment, reliability, and assembly throughout the semester.
